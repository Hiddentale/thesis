    \subsection{Eigenvectors and their eigenvalues}
        
        \begin{definition}
            Let \(T\) be a linear operator on a vector space \(V\). A nonzero vector \(v \in V\) is called an \emph{eigenvector} of \(T\) if there exists a scalar \(\lambda\) such that \(T(v) = \lambda v\). The scalar \(\lambda\) is called the \emph{eigenvalue} corresponding to the eigenvector \(v\) \cite[p.~246]{Insel}.
        \end{definition}
        Eigenvectors, in the realm of linear algebra, are non-zero vectors that maintain their direction under a linear transformation. This is typically represented by the equation \(Av = \lambda v\), where A is a matrix representing the linear transformation, \(v\) is the eigenvector, and \(\lambda\) is the corresponding eigenvalue, which is the factor by which the eigenvector is stretched.
        Matrices can be viewed as a representation of linear transformations. These transformations can involve operations like rotation, stretching, or shearing of vectors. 
        Eigenvectors are particularly significant because they provide a static reference point that remains unchanged under any transformation. This makes them invaluable for understanding how linear transformations behave on specific objects. Even if the initial plane undergoes a transformation such as stretching or rotation, its eigenvectors remain fixed, allowing us to easily comprehend the effects of the transformation.
       
    \subsection{The Characteristic Polynomial}
    \label{polynomial}
        The Characteristic Polynomial is the first step in getting a greater understanding of matrices in general.
        \begin{definition}
            Let \(A\) be a square matrix, then \(f(\lambda) = \det(A - \lambda I)\) is its characteristic polynomial \cite[p.~248]{Insel}.
        \end{definition}
        We are interested in applying this operation to our matrix \(A\) because it gives us a very easy way to figure out its eigenvalues. An example of the characteristic polynomial in practice can be seen in the \hyperref[char]{introduction} of this paper.
        
    \subsection{T-Invariant subspaces}
        \(T\)-invariant subspaces are subspaces of a vector space that are completely self-contained on transformation. Thus any vector in the subspace, under a certain transformation \(T\) will always transform into another vector of that same subspace. More formally:
    \begin{definition}
        Let \(T\) be a linear operator on a vector space \(V\). A subspace \(W\) of \(V\) is called a \(T\)-invariant subspace of \(V\) if \(T(W) \subseteq W\), that is, if \(T(v) \in W\) for all \(v \in W\) \cite[p.~313]{Insel}.
    \end{definition}
    By dividing a vector space, if possible, into T-invariant subspaces, we allow ourselves to study each T-invariant subspace independently. In general, it is easier to study multiple smaller independent vector spaces compared to one larger vector space, thus this makes our job easier.\par
    We can then define
    $$W = span({x, T(x), T^2(x), ...}),$$
    to be the \(T\)-cyclic subspace of \(V\), generated by \(x\).

    \subsection{Linear dependence}

            Let \(S\) be a linearly independent subset of a vector space \(V\), and let \(v\) be a vector in \(V\) that is not in \(S\). Then \(S \cup \{v\}\) is linearly dependent if and only if \(v \in span(S)\) \cite[p.~39]{Insel}.
        \end{theorem}

    \subsection{Division of the characteristic polynomial by its T-invariant subspace}
        \begin{theorem} \label{theorem 3.2.1}
        \setlength\marginparwidth{100pt}
        Let \(T\) be a linear operator on a finite-dimensional vector space \(V\), and let \(W\) be a \(T\)-invariant subspace of \(V\). Then the characteristic polynomial of \(T_W\), that is, the restriction of \(T\) to only \(W\) divides the characteristic polynomial of \(T\) \cite[p.~314]{Insel}.
        \end{theorem}
        \textit{Proof.} We choose an ordered basis  \(\alpha = \{v_1, v_2, \dotsc , v_k\}\) of \(W\). Then since \(W\) is a subspace of \(V\), we can extend \(\alpha\) such that it becomes an ordered basis for \(V\), \(\beta = \{v_1, v_2, \dotsc, v^k, v^{k+1}, \dotsc, v^n\}\). If we let \(A = [T]_\beta\) and \(B_1 = [T_W]_\alpha\), then it follows that since \(W\) is a \(T\)-invariant subspace of \(V\), by definition, \(T(v_i) \in W\) for all \(v_i \in \alpha\). Thus the first \(v_1\) to \(v_k\) bases in \(\beta\) are only used to represent \(W\), and as a result, \(A\) can be uniquely represented as a matrix with a zero in one corner: $$A = \begin{pmatrix}
            B_1 & B_2 \\ 
            0 & B_3
        \end{pmatrix}.$$
        If we then let \(f(t)\), \(g(t)\) be the respective characteristic polynomial of T and \(T_W\), it is easily derived that $$f(t) = \det(A-tI_n) = \det\begin{pmatrix}
            B_1 - tI_k & B_2 \\ 
            0 & B_3 -tI_{n-k}
        \end{pmatrix}, $$
        $$ = \det(B_1 - tI_k)\ \cdot\ \det(B_3 - tI_{n-k}) = g(t)\ \cdot\ \det(B_3 - tI_{n-k}),$$
        and consequently, $$\frac{f(t)}{g(t)} = \det(B_3 - tI_{n-k}),$$
        dividing 
        $$f(t).\quad\qedsymbol$$

    \subsection{\(T\)-cyclic subspaces and their bases}
        \begin{theorem}  \label{theorem 3.3.1} \cite[p.~315]{Insel}
            Let \(T\) be a linear operator on a finite-dimensional vector space V, and let W denote the \(T\)-cyclic subspace of \(V\) generated by a nonzero vector \(v \in V\). Let \(k = \dim(W)\). Then 
            \begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
                \item \({T^0(v), T(v), T^2(v),\ \dotsc\ , T^{k - 1}(v)}\), the collection of multiples of \(T(v)\), is a basis for W. 
                \item  If \(a_0v +a_1T(v)+\ \dotsc\ +a_{k - 1}T^{k - 1}(v)+T^k(v) = 0\), then the characteristic polynomial of \(T_W\) is \(f(t) = (-1)^k(a_0 + a_1t + \dotsc + a_{k - 1}t^{k - 1}+ t^k)\).
            \end{enumerate}
        \end{theorem}
        \textit{Proof.} \par
        (a) 
        Given a non-zero vector \(v\) in a finite-dimensional vector space \(V\), we can form a set 
        $$\beta = \{v, T(v), T^2(v),\ \dotsc\  , T^{j-1}(v)\},$$
        where \(T\) is a linear transformation and \(j\) is the largest positive integer such that \(\beta\) is linearly independent. We then define \(Z\) as the span of \(\beta\).\par
        Since \(\beta\) is a subset of \(Z\), it is clear that \(Z\) is a \(T\)-invariant subspace of \(V\). Furthermore if \(w \in Z\), then \(w\) can be expressed as a linear combination of the vectors in \(\beta\). Therefore, applying the linear transformation \(T\) to \(w\) results in a vector that is a linear combination of the vectors in \(Z\), implying that \(T(w)\) is also in \(Z\).\par
        We also know that \(T^j(v) \in Z\) according to Theorem \ref{theorem 3.1.1}. Furthermore, \(v \in Z\) as it is a linear combination of the vectors in \(\beta\).
        Therefore, \(Z\) is the smallest \(T\)-invariant subspace of \(V\) that contains \(v\), denoted as \(W\). It follows that \(Z\) is a subset of \(W\) and \(W\) is a subset of \(Z\). Hence, \(Z\) and W must be equal, and \(\beta\) forms a basis for \(W\).
        This implies that \(\dim(W) = j.\) and thus \(j = k\).\par
        (b)
        Now view \(\beta\), from (a), as an ordered basis for \(W\). Let \(a_0, a_1, \dotsc, a_{k - 1}\) be the scalars such that 
        $$a_0v + a_1T(v) + \dotsc + a_{k - 1}T^{k - 1}(v) + T^k(v) = 0.$$ 
        Observe that
        $$[T_W]_\beta = \begin{pmatrix}
            0 & 0 & \dotsc & 0 & -a_0 \\ 
            1 & 0 & \dotsc & 0 & -a_1 \\
            0 & 1 & \dotsc & 0 & -a_2 \\
            \vdots & \vdots &  & \vdots & \vdots \\
            0 & 0 & \dotsc & 1 & -a_{k-1}
        \end{pmatrix},$$
        which by some  induction on \(k\), which is outside the scope of this paper, gives us a characteristic polynomial
        $$f(t) = ( - 1)^k(a_0 + a_1t +\ \dotsc\  + a_{k - 1}t^{k - 1} + t^k).\quad\qedsymbol$$
        
    \subsection{Cayley-Hamilton}
        We now prove the Cayley-Hamilton Theorem \ref{theorem 1.1}.\par
        \textit{Proof.} We aim to demonstrate that for any vector \(v \in V\), the function \(f(T)(v) = 0\). \newline
        Given that T is a linear transformation, we can set \(v = 0\) to show that \(f(T)(0) = f(0) = 0\). This outcome is a direct result of the properties of vector spaces, where the zero vector, having no length or direction, remains at the origin regardless of any applied transformations.\newline
        Now suppose \(v \neq 0\), let's consider \(W\) as a \(T\)-cyclic subspace produced by \(v\), and assume the dimension of \(W\) is \(k\).
        Then it follows from Theorem \ref{theorem 3.3.1}(a) that there exist scalars \(a_0, a_1,\ \dotsc\ , a_{k-1}\) such that $$a_ov + a_1T(v) +\ \dotsc \ + a_{k-1}T^{k-1}(v) + T^k(v) = 0.$$
        Subsequently, by Theorem \ref{theorem 3.3.1}(b), the characteristic polynomial of \(T_W\) is:
        $$g(t) = (-1)^k(a_0 + a_1t + \dotsc + a_{k-1}t^{k-1} + t^k).$$
        When we combine these two equations, we find that:
        $$g(T)(v) = (-1)^k(a_0I + a_1T + \dotsc + a_{k-1}T^{k-1} + T^k)(v) = 0,$$
        This equation indicates that, as per Theorem \ref{theorem 3.2.1}, given \(g(t)\) is a divisor of \(f(t)\), there must exist a polynomial \(q(t)\) such that \(f(t)\) = \(q(t)g(t)\) and thus finally
        $$f(T)(v) = q(T)g(T)(v) = q(T)(g(T)(v)) = q(T)(0) = 0.\quad\qedsymbol$$

\section{Consequences of the Cayley-Hamilton Theorem}

    \subsection{Powers of matrices}
    The Cayley-Hamilton theorem establishes a fundamental relationship between a matrix and its powers. This relationship allows for the simplification of expressions involving these powers, enabling their evaluation without the need to calculate the power of the matrix or any higher powers.
    Consider an example where \(A\) is a  matrix
    $$A = \begin{pmatrix}
     2 & 4 \\ 
     2 & 1   
    \end{pmatrix}.$$
     According to the Cayley-Hamilton theorem, we have
     $$f(t) = (2-t)(1-t) - 8 = t^2 -3t -6 = 0,$$
     it follows that
     $$f(A) = A^2 -3A - 6I = 0,$$
     and thus
     $$A^2 = 3A + 6I.$$
     To calculate \(A^3\), we multiply \(A^2\) by A:
     $$A^3 = A^2A =(3A + 6I)A = 3A^2 + 6A = 3(3A+6I) + 6A = 15A + 36I,$$
     thus making it very easy for us to find higher powers of matrices in an alternative way \cite[p.~318]{Insel}.

    \subsection{Inverses of matrices}
    In the same way as the previous argument, we can also easily find the inverse of any square matrix by applying the Cayley-Hamilton Theorem.
    Again consider the same example, let \(A\) be a \(2\times2\) matrix
    $$A = \begin{pmatrix}
     2 & 4 \\ 
     2 & 1   
    \end{pmatrix}.$$
    Then
    $$f(A) = A^2 -3A - 6I = 0,$$
    and thus 
    $$6I = A^2 - 3A,\quad 6I = A(A-3), \quad A^{-1} = \frac{1}{6}(A-3) = \frac{A}{6} - \frac{1}{2}.$$
    It then follows that 
    $$A^{-1} =  \begin{pmatrix}
     \dfrac{-1}{6} & \dfrac{1}{6} \\ 
     \dfrac{-1}{6} & \dfrac{-1}{3}   
    \end{pmatrix}.$$
